This project will examine how evolutionary game theory can be used to model microbial competition in environments with limited resources. In this setting, the population consists of two competing organisms, each of which adopts one of two distinct inherited survival strategies. The success of each strategy depends both on the surrounding population and on the condition of the environment, particularly the availability of resources, which results in a modeling of the setting as repeated strategic interactions.

Within this project, we will study the survivability and long-term dynamics of two simulated microbial strains with varying strategies. Each strain will be assigned an initial strategy which will vary in each test of the simulation, and interactions between strains will be represented through a payoff matrix that measures fitness or reproductive success. We will then use these payoffs to model how the frequencies of the two strategies change over time within the overall population. As simulations are repeated, strategies with higher average payoffs will become more common, allowing us to observe whether the system trends toward domination by one strain or strategy and what balance is established.

The main goal of the project is to determine how varying strategies influence the long-term evolutionary outcome of the populations. In particular, we want to test whether there exists a state in which two microbial strategies can coexist in a predictable balance, and whether that balance can be explained mathematically using a payoff matrix model.
